My senior year at UC Santa Cruz entailed a year-long series of classes centering about the development of a polished game: Children of Àṣẹ, a top-down social dynamics RPG set within the Yoruba culture of what today is Nigeria. I was one of nine developers, working with a core team of around five artists – a total of 27 people contributed in all. I handled much of the OpenGL rendering, asset integration, and tutorial design. We spent twenty weeks in all on the game, divided into two-week sprints and holding Scrum three times each week.

Unique among the games to come out of the class of 2017, we chose to build our own engine with Visual C++. At the annual Sammy Awards for undergraduate and graduate games at UCSC, we were a runner-up for the technical achievement award.

Though the game's graphics are two-dimensional, all the human characters were animated as 3D meshes by Jude, our main 3D artist, to be rendered into 2D spritesheets. This gave the characters a consistent look regardless of the direction they might face.

Finally, we also consulted with a babalawo – a Yoruba spiritual leader – to ensure our representation of the Yoruba world was accurate and culturally sensitive.
